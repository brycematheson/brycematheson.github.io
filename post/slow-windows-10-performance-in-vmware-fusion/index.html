    • Slow Windows 10 Performance in VMWare Fusion

    January 25, 2016

    When Windows 10 was released in July, I had a little bit of a bad taste in my mouth and a sense that it just wasn’t quite “finished” yet. It seemed to me that it was lacking the polish that really completed the project and made it a viable choice for use as a daily OS. Turns out my bad first impression was mostly due to that fact that I was running it through VMWare Fusion on my Mac. You can read my initial impressions here.

    After being forced to upgrade to Windows 10 on desktop at work, I realized that I actually really enjoyed Windows 10. I didn’t experience any of the lag or performance issues that I had on my Mac back home, even though my MacBook is newer and faster.

    I’ve been too busy to be able to revisit the issue and try to get a version that I’m actually content with on my MacBook Pro. I had no issues whatsoever running Windows 7 or Windows 8 as a VM through VMWare Fusion and performance wasn’t an issue at all, even with just 1 or 2GB of memory allocated to it. I don’t use it for any hardcore video editing or anything, but just the occasional Windows app that doesn’t play nicely with Mac OS X.

    This weekend, I finally had a chance to play around with a bit more. Previously, Windows 10 on VMWare would be painfully slow. Beyond the point of being usable.

    I’ve found a “sort of” workaround. I installed the 32-bit version of Windows 10 (on accident), and was surprised that it didn’t seem to suffer (as badly) with lag as the 64-bit version did. It’s still not great, and it’s still not the same as running it natively on its own, but at least it’s now usable. I’ve also disabled “3D Acceleration” in the Display Settings, as well as Folder Sharing. Doing these three things together makes for a usable box.

    Now, you may be thinking, “Really? You’re going to make me use a 32-bit OS in 2016? That’s soooooo 10 years ago.” Yes, well, deal with it. For me, the main benefits I get from running a 64-bit OS is being able to take full advantage of memory (as we all know that 32-bit OS’s can only use a maximum of 3.5GB). While this would normally be an issue, it isn’t for me, as I never allocate more than 2GB of RAM to my VMs. My MacBook only has a total of 8GB, so I don’t want to starve my main OS of resources.

    Installing VM Tools and updating to the latest build of Windows 10 (1511, released in November) has seemed to improve speed as well.

    Again, it’s not as responsive and speedy as I would like it to be, but it’s at least usable until VMWare can figure out their crap.
